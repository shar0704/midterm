Its summer time madness

The history of the bikini can be traced back to as early as 5600 BC, with evidence of bikini-style women's clothing found during that era. However, the modern bikini, as we know it today, was introduced on July 5, 1946, by French engineer Louis Réard. It was named after the Bikini Atoll, where the first post-war tests of the atomic bomb were taking place.

Initially, the bikini faced resistance from various quarters, including the Catholic Church, some media outlets, and a majority of the public who considered it risqué or scandalous. However, over time, it gained acceptance, and famous personalities like Brigitte Bardot, Rita Hayworth, and Ava Gardner helped popularize it through their public appearances in bikinis.

The bikini received additional legitimacy when it appeared on the covers of popular magazines like Playboy and Sports Illustrated in the early 1960s. It also gained iconic status with memorable moments in movies, such as Ursula Andress emerging from the surf in a bikini in the James Bond film Dr. No and Raquel Welch wearing a deer skin bikini in the film One Million Years B.C.

As society progressed and women's emancipation advanced, the bikini gained wide acceptance in Western society. It became the most popular type of female beachwear globally, owing to the empowerment of women, according to French fashion historian Olivier Saillard. By the early 2000s, the bikini industry had become a lucrative business, generating billions of dollars annually and even boosting related services like bikini waxing and sun tanning
